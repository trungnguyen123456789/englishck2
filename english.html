Read the following passage about endangered species and mark the letter A, B, C or D.

There are more than 8 million animal and plant species in the world today. However, many of them are on the brink of extinction. According to the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), over 40,000 species are currently endangered. This includes iconic animals like tigers, elephants, and rhinos. But what about lesser-known species? Many amphibians, insects, and plants are disappearing at an alarming rate. Since the 1970s, the population of monitored vertebrate species has declined by 69%. When a species becomes extinct, the consequences go beyond the loss of that species. It disrupts ecosystems, leading to a chain reaction that can affect other species, including humans. For example, the decline of bees threatens global food production, as they are essential for pollinating crops. Conservation efforts have been implemented worldwide. Wildlife reserves and national parks provide protected areas where species can live without human interference. Governments and organizations are also focusing on breeding programs to increase the population of endangered animals. One such program is aimed at saving the California condor, which had fewer than 30 individuals left in the wild in the 1980s but now has a population of over 300. Technology provides another avenue for protecting endangered species. Satellite tracking helps monitor migration patterns, while drones are used to patrol protected areas and prevent poaching. Moreover, genetic research is being explored to understand how to increase species resilience. With growing awareness and collective action, there is hope that we can prevent further extinctions and preserve biodiversity for future generations.

[ I ] The rise of remote work has transformed the modern workplace, offering flexibility and a better “work-life balance” for employees around the world (Bloom et al., 2015). [ II ] This trend, accelerated by technological advancements and the COVID-19 pandemic, allows employees to work from home or any location with internet access (Gajendran & Harrison, 2007). [ III ] While remote work provides numerous benefits, it also presents challenges that organizations and employees must address to remain productive and engaged. [ IV ] One of the main benefits of remote work is increased flexibility. Employees can manage their time more effectively, which often leads to higher job satisfaction and reduced stress. Furthermore, remote work eliminates the need for daily commutes, saving time and reducing transportation expenses. Many employees report feeling more empowered to control their schedules, allowing them to better balance personal and professional responsibilities. However, remote work also comes with significant challenges. Isolation is a common issue, as employees miss out on face-to-face interactions and team bonding, which are important for mental well-being and collaboration. Studies have shown that prolonged isolation can lead to feelings of loneliness, reducing job satisfaction and affecting mental health (Ozcelik & Barsade, 2018). Additionally, remote workers may struggle to maintain boundaries between work and personal life, leading to overwork and burnout. To make remote work successful, companies need to implement strategies that support employee well-being and productivity. Providing tools for virtual collaboration, setting clear work-life boundaries, and encouraging regular team check-ins can help mitigate the challenges of remote work. By balancing flexibility with structured support, organizations can create a sustainable remote work environment that benefits both employers and employees (Larson & DeChurch, 2020).
